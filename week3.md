## Content Insights

This week I learned that WordPress.org and wordpress.com are not the same thing. WordPress.org is the open-source CMS software that can be installed on a hosting service or local server, while wordpress.com is a hosted website builder service. For this practical, it is important to use the normal WordPress.org system, not wordpress.com.

Compared with Joomla, WordPress felt easier to understand at the beginning. Creating pages, changing themes, and editing content with the block editor was more visual and straightforward. However, I also noticed that WordPress still has many settings, and it is easy to make the website look unprofessional if the theme, images, menus, and page structure are not planned carefully.

I learned that a basic WordPress startup website should still have a clear structure. For example, my startup website can include:

- Home page: introduces the startup idea and main value
- About page: explains the purpose of the business
- Services or Features page: explains what the startup provides
- Contact page: provides contact or enquiry information

I also learned that themes control the general appearance of a WordPress website, but content still needs to be organised properly. A good theme does not automatically make a good website. The website still needs suitable text, images, headings, navigation, and layout.

Another important concept was plugins. Plugins can add extra functions to a WordPress site, such as contact forms, SEO tools, security tools, and backup tools. However, I learned that adding too many plugins can make a site slower or harder to manage. This means plugins should be chosen carefully based on actual needs.

The group-formation activity also helped me understand that team structure matters before the project even begins. Good group members are not only people with technical skills. Availability, communication, reliability, and shared expectations are also very important.

During the interviews, I noticed that students had different working styles. Some preferred to complete tasks early, while others were more comfortable working closer to the deadline. Some people were confident with coding or design, while others were better at writing, organising tasks, or communicating with the team. This helped me see that a balanced group should include different strengths, not just similar skills.

## Career/Employability/Learning Insights

This week helped me connect CMS skills with real workplace expectations. WordPress is widely used by small businesses, organisations, blogs, and online stores, so learning how to build and manage a WordPress site is useful for employability. Even if I do not become a WordPress developer, understanding CMS platforms can help me work with clients, update websites, and understand how real websites are maintained.

I also realised that WordPress work is not only about installing a theme. A useful WordPress developer or website manager needs to understand content structure, user experience, responsive design, basic security, plugins, and deployment. This made me understand that CMS skills are practical workplace skills, not just classroom tasks.

The group interview activity was useful because it felt similar to a real job interview or team selection process. I learned that choosing group members should not only be based on friendship. It is better to ask practical questions about availability, communication, task responsibility, and expectations. This can reduce problems later in the project.

One thing I learned about myself is that I prefer working with people who communicate clearly and start tasks early. In previous group work, problems often happened when people did not reply or waited until the last minute. Because of this, I think it is important to choose teammates who are honest about their schedule and willing to update the group regularly.

I also noticed that I need to improve how I explain my own strengths during interviews. When other students asked what I could contribute, I realised I should be more prepared to describe my skills clearly. For example, I can say that I am willing to help with website setup, documentation, writing, and practical problem-solving. This is useful for future employment because interviews often require people to explain their skills confidently and specifically.

This week showed me that technical ability and teamwork ability are both important. A project can fail even if the group has good technical skills, especially if communication is poor or expectations are unclear. For the upcoming group project, I should try to communicate earlier, record decisions clearly, and make sure everyone understands their role.
